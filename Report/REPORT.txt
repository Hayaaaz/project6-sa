1. Introduction
In this project, I walk through how a single NVIDIA H100 can be safely shared across four very different university workloads. My goal wasn’t just to “slice the GPU,” but to show that I understand why each workload belongs in its specific place, how MIG enforces isolation, and how to defend those decisions.

Because I’m working in a simulated environment, I can’t run real VRAM measurements or carve an actual H100. Instead, I follow the guidance in Chapter 4 — especially the reminder that VRAM usage is more than just weights and that the KV cache often dominates. Using that framework, I choose reasonable MIG profiles, prove isolation using simulated UUIDs, and demonstrate correct pinning behavior.

One workload matters more than all the others: the registrar’s FERPA‑protected classifier. If this workload is not placed on a hardware‑isolated MIG slice, the Normal tier is not met — no matter how elegant the rest of the partition looks. I keep that requirement front‑and‑center throughout the design.

6. Choosing the Correct Sharing Mode  
Chapter 6 defines four GPU‑sharing modes:

Passthrough (VFIO) — one VM gets the entire GPU

Time‑slicing — GPU is shared in round‑robin slices

MPS (Multi‑Process Service) — best‑effort concurrent sharing

MIG (Multi‑Instance GPU) — hardware‑isolated GPU instances
______________________________

My choices:

Registrar (FERPA)  
MIG (hardware‑isolated slice)  
FERPA requires true hardware isolation. Time‑slicing and MPS do not provide isolation.

Library  
MIG slice  
Predictable latency and a steady embedding workload.

CS Department  
MIG slice (3g.40gb)  
Larger conversational model; time‑slicing or MPS would cause latency spikes.

Research Student  
Time‑slicing  
Non‑critical, exploratory notebook workload.
